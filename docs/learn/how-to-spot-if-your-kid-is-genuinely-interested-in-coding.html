How to spot if your kid is genuinely interested in coding

Why This Question Is Worth Asking Carefully

Plenty of kids say they want to try coding because a friend mentioned it, because a teacher suggested it, or because they saw it on a YouTube channel for five minutes. That's fine — curiosity has to start somewhere. But genuine interest looks different from polite enthusiasm, and knowing the difference saves you money on courses, saves your kid from sitting through something they resent, and helps you actually nurture something real if it's there.

This isn't about gatekeeping. It's about reading the signals accurately before you commit to anything.

What Genuine Interest Actually Looks Like

They Get Frustrated When Something Doesn't Work — And Keep Going Anyway

This is probably the clearest early signal. A kid who is genuinely interested in making things with code doesn't just quit when a project breaks. They get annoyed, maybe walk away for twenty minutes, and then come back trying a different approach. That tolerance for frustration-plus-persistence is foundational to coding, and it shows up early in kids who have the genuine pull toward it.

Compare this to a kid who loses interest the moment something goes wrong. That's not necessarily a character flaw — it might just mean the subject hasn't clicked in the right way yet, or isn't the right fit at all.

They Ask "How Does This Work?" About Specific Things

Broad curiosity is common in children. What's rarer is specific curiosity about mechanisms. If your kid watches a video game cutscene and asks how the characters move, or uses a website and wonders how the button knows what to do when you click it, or asks why the traffic light changes in that order — that mechanistic thinking is a predictor.

It doesn't have to be explicitly about computers. Kids who want to understand how systems work — how rules produce outcomes — are temperamentally suited to coding logic.

They've Already Started Messing Around on Their Own

This is the strongest signal of all. Before any structured course or class, have they opened Scratch, or followed a YouTube tutorial on their own, or modified something in Minecraft's settings to see what would happen? Self-directed exploration, even shallow, tells you the pull is intrinsic rather than external.

If the answer is no and they've had access to the internet and a device, that's worth noting. It doesn't mean they can't develop genuine interest, but it means you're at the planting stage, not the watering stage.

They Have Projects in Their Head

Ask your kid: "If you knew how to code, what would you make?"

A kid with genuine interest has an answer ready — probably too many answers. They want to make a game where you control a dragon, or an app that helps them track their Pokémon card collection, or a website for their favourite sports team. The specificity matters. They've been imagining already.

A kid who's just going along with the idea usually says something vague like "I don't know, maybe a game?" and trails off. That's not disqualifying, but it tells you you're at an earlier stage than you might think.

Signs That Might Look Like Interest But Aren't

Enthusiasm for Playing Games vs. Making Games

This is the most common source of confusion. A kid who loves video games isn't automatically drawn to making them. The experience of playing a game is almost entirely opposite to the experience of building one — one is instant gratification, the other is sustained, often boring, detail work that pays off only much later.

Watch for the specific shift: do they ever look at a game and think about the inside of it? Do they notice glitches and wonder why they happen? Interest in the construction, not just the product, is what matters here.

Saying Yes Because They Think You Want Them To

Children are often remarkably good at reading parental enthusiasm and reflecting it back. If you've talked excitedly about how coding is important for the future, your child may have enthusiastically agreed — and genuinely believed their own enthusiasm in the moment — without it being a deep interest.

A simple test: bring it up in a neutral, low-stakes way. "We could sign you up for that coding club, or you could just carry on with your art class — what do you actually want?" and watch their face before they have time to construct an answer.

Short Bursts of Enthusiasm That Don't Persist

Some kids go through phases where they want to try everything intensely for two weeks. If coding is in rotation alongside wanting to learn guitar, become a chef, and take up fencing — all in the same month — you're probably looking at a naturally curious kid rather than one with a specific coding pull. That's wonderful, but it means you should test with low commitment first.

How to Test the Water Without Over-Investing

Give Them an Unstructured Hour

Point them toward a free, open-ended environment — something block-based or simple — and leave them alone with it. Don't set a goal. Just say "see what you can make." Come back in an hour.

What happens during that hour matters more than what they produce. Did they explore different options? Did they hit a problem and try to solve it? Did they call you over to show you something, or did they quietly drift to something else? Genuine interest shows up in self-direction.

Let Them Lead the Goal

If they show any interest, ask them what they want to build. Then help them find the simplest possible version of that thing. If they want to make a quiz game, help them find the absolute most basic way to do that. Then step back.

The kids who are genuinely hooked will take the bones of what you showed them and start extending it, breaking it, rebuilding it. The kids who aren't quite there yet will wait for the next instruction. Both responses are useful information.

Try Two or Three Different Formats

Some kids who don't engage with visual block coding light up when they see actual text-based code. Others are the opposite. Some love game-building contexts; others are more interested in making websites. If the first format you try produces nothing, it doesn't mean coding isn't for them — it might just mean that format isn't the entry point.

Age-Specific Things to Watch For

Ages 6–9

At this age, the key signals are: do they like giving instructions? Do they enjoy sequencing things (first this, then this, then this)? Do they notice cause-and-effect relationships and seem satisfied when they work out why something happened? Abstract logic is not on the table yet, but the foundations of computational thinking absolutely are.

Ages 10–13

By this age, you can look for more specific signals: interest in modding games, building in sandbox environments, following tutorials independently, or asking increasingly specific "how does this work" questions about digital things. This is also when the frustration tolerance test becomes most revealing — projects get complex enough that persistence really matters.

Ages 14+

At this age, genuine interest tends to be self-evident because they've likely already pursued it independently to some degree. If a teenager says they want to learn coding and hasn't explored any of it on their own despite having the opportunity, the question worth asking is why they want it — is it external pressure, or is there a specific thing they want to build or do?

Common Pitfalls When Assessing Interest

Confusing performance with passion. A child can be quite good at a beginner coding exercise without being interested in coding. Kids are quick learners and often perform well at things they're indifferent to.

Projecting your own timeline. Some kids need exposure before they develop interest. Seeing what's possible, at a demonstration or a class, can flip a switch. Don't interpret "no interest yet" as "no interest ever."

Making it high-stakes. The moment coding becomes something they're being tested on or judged for, it stops telling you about interest and starts telling you about performance anxiety. Keep it casual, especially early.

Deciding based on one session. One bad Saturday afternoon where they were tired and didn't engage doesn't mean coding isn't for them. One excited afternoon where they made something simple doesn't mean they'll sustain it. Look for patterns over weeks, not conclusions from single data points.

FAQ

My kid says they want to learn coding but gets bored after ten minutes. Is that normal? Very normal at the beginning, especially if what they're doing doesn't connect to something they care about. Try framing coding around a specific project they actually want to build — boredom often means the task feels abstract, not that the interest is absent.

Is it too early to tell if my seven-year-old has genuine interest? At seven, you're really looking at early indicators — do they like building things, do they enjoy following sequences, do they persist when puzzles get hard? You can't read deep interest at that age, but you can see whether the underlying traits are there.

My daughter isn't interested in games. Does that mean coding isn't for her? Not at all. Coding has as much to do with making websites, analysing data, creating art tools, building simulations, or automating tedious tasks as it does with games. If game-focused coding leaves her cold, try a different context entirely.

How long should I give it before concluding it's not the right fit? Three to four genuine attempts — different formats, different projects, different entry points — spread over a few months, is a reasonable sample. If none of them have produced any spark of self-directed curiosity, it's fair to move on without guilt.
